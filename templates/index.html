Russian aerial attacks on Ukraine - Data Dashboard

Data on attacks launched against Ukraine in Feb 2022 - Apr 2025

Unable to achieve success on the battlefield, Russia significantly stepped up its aerial attacks targeting Ukraine’s energy and civil infrastructure in an apparent shift to weaken the country’s resilience and morale.

The data shows a sharp rise in attacks in 2023, peaking in 2024 with over double the number from the previous year. This surge highlights a deliberate strategy to maximize disruption during critical periods, especially winter.

[TIP: De-select Shahed for better view of other weapons]

Iranian-designed Shahed drones and X-101/X-555 cruise missiles launched by strategic bombers over the Caspian Sea remain Russia’s most frequently deployed weapons against Ukraine. However, due to their limited effectiveness against Ukraine’s increasingly capable air defenses, Russia has turned to more sophisticated weaponry. The use of the extremely costly Kinzhal hypersonic missiles has doubled, while the deployment of the newer Iskander-class ballistic missiles has increased fivefold. In Q1 2025 Russia launched as many Shaheds as in the whole year of 2024 having scaled thier production.

Ukraine’s air defense continues to perform at a highly effective level. The decline observed in 2024 is largely attributed to a sharp increase in the number of Russian attacks—nearly double that of the previous year—combined with the inherent limitations of any air defense system, none of which can guarantee a 100% interception rate. Additionally, delays in the approval of a U.S. military aid package by Congress further impacted performance. The continued decline into 2025 is primarily the result of a temporary suspension of American military assistance to Ukraine

Ironically, the predominantly Russian-speaking city of Odesa remains a primary target of Russian aerial assaults. Kyiv and Lviv face attacks with comparable frequency. Due to their proximity to the frontline, Dnipro and Kharkiv are subjected to both long-range missile strikes and short-range weapons, such as glide bombs and X-22 missiles

In 2024 alone, Russia spent more on aerial attacks against Ukraine than it did on healthcare for its entire population. Cumulatively over the past three years, the cost of these aerial attacks exceeds $35bn, which is nearly 2% of Russia’s GDP
